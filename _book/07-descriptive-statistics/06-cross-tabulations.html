97  Cross-Tabulations

The Problem

You know 25% of all participants smoke (previous page) — but your professor’s actual question is more specific: “Does the smoking rate differ between men and women?” A single overall percentage can’t answer this; you need to look at smoking status separately within each sex — exactly the kind of two-variable breakdown table() alone (with one argument) can’t produce.

Your Mission

Build a two-way frequency table — a cross-tabulation — showing how two categorical variables relate to each other.

Think Before Coding

  • From Level 5, count(participants, sex, smoker) produced counts for every sex-and-smoking-status combination as separate rows. Could table() produce something similar, but shaped like a grid instead?
  • If you wanted to know “what percentage of women smoke” specifically, would you divide by the total number of participants, or just the total number of women?

97.1 The R Concept

table() with two arguments builds a cross-tabulation — a grid with one variable’s categories as rows and the other’s as columns:

table(row_variable, column_variable)
table(participants$sex, participants$smoker)

To convert this into row-wise percentages (e.g., “of all women, what percent smoke?”), use prop.table() with margin = 1 (percentages within each row):

prop.table(table(participants$sex, participants$smoker), margin = 1)

97.2 Demonstration

participants <- read.csv("data/participants.csv")

sex_smoker_table <- table(participants$sex, participants$smoker)
sex_smoker_table
        No Yes
Female  42  10
Male    33  15

Now you can see, at a glance, that among the 52 women, 42 don’t smoke and 10 do — and among the 48 men, 33 don’t smoke and 15 do.

round(prop.table(sex_smoker_table, margin = 1) * 100, 1)
          No  Yes
Female  80.8 19.2
Male    68.8 31.2

margin = 1 computes each row’s percentages within that row — 80.8% and 19.2% for women sum to 100%, and 68.8% and 31.2% for men sum to 100% separately, rather than all four percentages summing to 100% together.

97.3 Explore

Try prop.table(sex_smoker_table) with no margin argument at all. Notice all four percentages now sum to 100% overall, answering a different question (“what percent of the entire study is female non-smokers?”) than the row-wise version above (“what percent of women don’t smoke?”).

97.4 Solve the Problem

participants <- read.csv("data/participants.csv")

sex_smoker_table <- table(participants$sex, participants$smoker)
sex_smoker_pct <- round(prop.table(sex_smoker_table, margin = 1) * 100, 1)

sex_smoker_table
        No Yes
Female  42  10
Male    33  15
sex_smoker_pct
          No  Yes
Female  80.8 19.2
Male    68.8 31.2

Yes — the smoking rate does differ meaningfully by sex in this sample: 31.2% of men smoke, compared to 19.2% of women, a 12-percentage-point difference directly answering your professor’s question.

97.5 Interpret the Result

Reporting the overall 25% smoking rate (previous page) alone would have hidden this sex difference entirely — the cross-tabulation reveals that men in this sample smoke at nearly double the rate of women. As always, this is a descriptive pattern in n=48 men and n=52 women, not yet a tested statistical claim (Level 11 covers how to test whether a difference like this is likely to reflect a real, generalizable pattern versus chance).

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting to specify margin and misreading the resulting percentages. Without margin = 1 or margin = 2, prop.table() computes percentages relative to the entire table, not within each row or column — a very different (and often not what you intended) question.
  • Confusing margin = 1 (row percentages) with margin = 2 (column percentages). margin = 1 makes each row sum to 100%; margin = 2 makes each column sum to 100% instead — mixing these up flips which variable you’re computing percentages “within.”
  • Drawing a causal conclusion from a descriptive cross-tabulation. “Men smoke more than women in this sample” is a valid descriptive finding — it is not, by itself, evidence that being male causes higher smoking rates; cross-tabulations describe patterns, they don’t establish causation.

97.6 Practice Problem

Build a cross-tabulation of county and smoker, then compute column percentages (margin = 2) to answer: “of all current smokers, what percentage live in each county?”

97.7 Challenge Problem

Use table(participants$sex, participants$smoker) together with margin = 2 (instead of margin = 1) to compute: “of all smokers, what percentage are male versus female?” Explain in words why this answers a different question than the row-percentage version already computed above.

97.8 Key Takeaways

  • table(var1, var2) builds a two-way cross-tabulation, showing how two categorical variables relate.
  • prop.table(cross_tab, margin = 1) computes row-wise percentages; margin = 2 computes column-wise percentages; omitting margin computes percentages relative to the whole table.
  • A cross-tabulation can reveal patterns (like a sex difference in smoking rate) that an overall single-variable summary completely hides — but it remains a descriptive finding, not a causal claim.

Knowledge Check

  1. Multiple choice. What does margin = 1 mean in prop.table(tab, margin = 1)?

    1. Percentages computed within each column
    2. Percentages computed within each row
    3. Percentages computed relative to the whole table
    4. It has no effect

  2. True or false. A cross-tabulation can reveal patterns that an overall single-variable frequency table would hide.

  3. Predict the output. In sex_smoker_table, do the row percentages for “Female” (80.8, 19.2) sum to 100?

  4. Short answer. Why is “men smoke more than women in this sample” a safer statement than “being male causes higher smoking rates,” based on this cross-tabulation alone?

Show answers
  1. b) Percentages computed within each row.
  2. True. The overall 25% smoking rate hid a substantial sex difference (19.2% vs. 31.2%) that only the cross-tabulation revealed.
  3. Yes — 80.8 + 19.2 = 100, exactly as expected for row percentages.
  4. A cross-tabulation only describes an association observed in this particular sample — it doesn’t rule out other explanations (like differences in age, county, or other unmeasured factors between the male and female participants) that a purely descriptive table can’t account for.
