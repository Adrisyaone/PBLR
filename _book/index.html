Welcome

Why this course exists

Most R textbooks teach syntax first and hope the motivation follows. This course does the opposite. Every page opens with a problem you might actually be handed as a public health intern, a research assistant, an epidemiology student, or a data analyst on a health department team — and you learn exactly the R you need, at the moment you need it, to solve it.

You will not find a chapter that starts with “Today we will learn the filter() function.” Instead you will find a supervisor asking you to find every participant over 65 with hypertension, and filter() will show up as the tool that gets you out of that jam.

Who this is for

This course assumes zero prior programming experience. If you have never written a line of code in your life but you can think through a problem logically, you are the target reader. It is built for students and professionals in:

Public health, epidemiology, biostatistics, clinical research, health data analysis, survey research, and population health.

By the final capstone level, the material assumes you have worked through everything before it — the course is cumulative, not a reference manual to jump around in on your first pass.

How each lesson works

Every lesson in this book follows the same rhythm, because the rhythm itself is what builds problem-solving skill:

1. Real-World Problem — a short, realistic scenario from health research.

2. Your Mission — the concrete task you need to accomplish.

3. Think Before Coding — a few questions to reason through before touching the keyboard.

4. The R Concept — the one new idea you need, explained plainly: what it does, its syntax, its arguments, and what usually goes wrong.

5. Demonstration — runnable R code showing the concept in isolation.

6. Explore — small modifications you make to that code to test your understanding.

7. Solve the Problem — you return to the opening scenario and solve it completely.

8. Interpret the Result — the output translated into a plain-language sentence a supervisor or reader would understand.

9. Common Mistakes — errors beginners reliably make with this concept, and why they happen.

10. Practice Problem — a new, similar problem for you to solve with support.

11. Challenge Problem — a harder variant you solve with less scaffolding.

12. Key Takeaways — two to four sentences worth remembering.

13. Knowledge Check — multiple-choice, true/false, predict-the-output, find-the-error, and write-the-code questions, with answers hidden behind a toggle so you can self-test honestly.

The dataset you’ll live in

Rather than switching example datasets every page, most of this course reuses a single running dataset: a simulated community health survey of 100 participants, with age, sex, county, systolic and diastolic blood pressure, BMI, and smoking status — including realistic missing values. You’ll meet it for the first time in Level 1 and return to it constantly, so that learning R and learning to think about a real dataset happen together. The file lives at data/participants.csv.

By Level 15 you’ll be running full analyses — and eventually independent capstone projects — on data structured just like it.

What you’ll be able to do

By the end, starting from zero, you will be able to navigate R and RStudio, write and debug your own R code, understand R’s core data structures, import and clean real-world health data, manipulate data fluently with the tidyverse, build publication-quality figures, write your own functions and automate repetitive analysis, run and interpret the statistical methods most common in health research, work with longitudinal, survey, and time-to-event data, and produce a fully reproducible research report from raw data to final results — in Quarto, from scratch.
