1  What Is R?

The Problem

You’ve just started as a public health intern at your local health department. On your first day, your supervisor says: “We collected survey data from 100 participants in our community health study. I need the average age reported by Friday. Everyone here uses R for this — go ahead and get started.”

You’ve used Excel before. You’ve never heard of R. You don’t even know if it’s software you install, a website, or a programming language.

Your Mission

Understand what R actually is, why a health department would use it instead of Excel, and what it means to “write code” to answer a data question.

Think Before Coding

  • When you’ve calculated an average before (in Excel, or by hand), what steps did you follow?
  • What happens in Excel if you need to repeat the exact same calculation on next month’s data — do you redo all your clicks?
  • What might be an advantage of writing down the steps of a calculation, rather than just clicking through a menu?

1.1 The R Concept

R is a free, open-source programming language built specifically for statistics, data analysis, and graphics. It was created by statisticians, for statisticians — which is why it’s the default choice in public health, epidemiology, biostatistics, and academic research more broadly.

A few things make R different from a spreadsheet:

  • You write instructions, not clicks. Instead of selecting a range of cells and clicking “AVERAGE,” you write a line of code that says, in effect, “take this data and compute its mean.” That line can be saved, reused, and rerun on next month’s data in seconds.
  • It’s reproducible. Anyone with your code and your data can get the exact same result. A colleague reviewing your Excel file has to guess which cells you clicked; a colleague reading your R script can see exactly what you did.
  • It’s built for data, not documents. R has no concept of a “sheet” with 500 unrelated things on it. It’s designed around clean, structured data and the operations researchers actually need: summarizing, filtering, modeling, plotting.
  • It’s free and extensible. R itself is free, and thousands of free add-ons (called packages) extend it — for reading messy Excel files, running survival analysis, mapping disease outbreaks, and far more.

R is not the app with the blue icon you click to open. That’s a separate program called RStudio, which you’ll meet on the next page. R is the language and engine underneath — RStudio is the workspace you’ll actually sit in.

1.2 Demonstration

Every R session, however small, starts with running code. Let’s run the simplest possible line: basic arithmetic.

# R can act as a calculator right out of the box
2 + 2
[1] 4

That [1] 4 is R’s answer. The [1] just means “this is result number 1” — you’ll see why that notation matters once you work with longer lists of numbers.

Here’s a second line, doing something closer to what your supervisor actually wants: averaging a few numbers.

# The average of four ages, computed directly
(34 + 51 + 62 + 29) / 4
[1] 44

1.3 Explore

Try changing the numbers in the calculation above to four different ages. Then try adding a fifth age to the sum — remember to also change the number you divide by. What happens if you forget to update the divisor?

1.4 Solve the Problem

You don’t yet have the tools to import your supervisor’s actual 100-participant file — that’s coming in a later page. But you can already answer the conceptual question your supervisor implicitly asked: is R the right tool for this, and what will doing this work in R actually look like?

Yes — R is exactly the right tool. Once your data is loaded into R (Level 1, later pages), computing a mean will take a single line of code, mean(), no matter how many participants there are. Whether it’s 100 or 100,000 rows, the code doesn’t get any harder.

1.5 Interpret the Result

When you ran 2 + 2 and got [1] 4, you weren’t just testing a calculator — you confirmed that R is installed, running, and ready to take instructions. That’s the same confirmation a much bigger analysis needs: R evaluates your code and hands back an answer, every time, the same way.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing R and RStudio. People often say “open R” when they mean “open RStudio.” You’ll always work inside RStudio, which runs R for you behind the scenes.
  • Assuming R is only for expert statisticians. R is used by beginners and experts alike — the language scales with you.
  • Expecting a menu-driven experience. If you’re used to Excel’s toolbar, the biggest adjustment is trusting that a line of text is the instruction, not a shortcut to one.

1.6 Practice Problem

List three tasks in your own field (or one you’re interested in — clinical research, survey analysis, health policy) where you currently rely on Excel or manual calculation, and that could instead be done with reusable, written instructions.

1.7 Challenge Problem

Write two or three sentences, in your own words, explaining to a classmate who has never coded why a health department might choose R over Excel for analyzing a 10,000-row patient dataset. Be specific about at least one concrete advantage.

1.8 Key Takeaways

  • R is a free, open-source programming language built for statistics and data analysis.
  • Working in R means writing instructions rather than clicking through menus — which makes your work reusable and reproducible.
  • R and RStudio are not the same thing; R is the language, RStudio is the workspace (next page).

Knowledge Check

  1. Multiple choice. R is best described as:

    1. A spreadsheet program
    2. A programming language for statistics and data analysis
    3. A database management system
    4. A word processor

  2. True or false. R and RStudio are two names for the exact same program.

  3. Predict the output. What will the following code print?

    10 * 3 - 5

  4. Write the code. Write one line of R code that calculates the average of the numbers 12, 18, and 24 without using any function — just arithmetic.

Show answers
  1. b) A programming language for statistics and data analysis.
  2. False. R is the underlying language; RStudio is a separate application (an IDE) that makes R easier to use.
  3. [1] 25 (10 × 3 = 30, then 30 − 5 = 25).
  4. (12 + 18 + 24) / 3, which evaluates to [1] 18.
