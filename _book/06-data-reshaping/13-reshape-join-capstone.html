91  Level 6 Capstone: Reshaping and Joining Together

The Problem

Your professor’s final question for this level ties everything together: “Does blood pressure change from visit 1 to visit 3 differ between younger and older participants?” Answering this needs two separate skills from this level at once — reshaping bp_visits_wide.csv to compute each participant’s change score (Page 7), and then joining that result against baseline_enrollment.csv to bring in each participant’s age, since age was never part of the visits file to begin with.

Your Mission

Chain reshaping and joining together in a single analysis: reshape to compute a per-participant value, then join in demographic information from a separate file to analyze it further.

Think Before Coding

  • bp_visits_wide.csv and baseline_enrollment.csv are two entirely separate files. What column do they share that could serve as a join key?
  • Not every participant in bp_visits_wide.csv will necessarily have a matching row in baseline_enrollment.csv (they’re different studies’ worth of ids). Which join type keeps every visits-file participant regardless of whether a baseline match exists?

91.1 The R Concept

Real analyses frequently need both reshaping and joining — reshape one dataset into an analysis-ready shape, then join in additional variables from an entirely different source before the real analysis can happen:

df |>
  pivot_longer(...) |>          # reshape to long
  filter(...) |>
  pivot_wider(...) |>            # reshape back to wide, aligned for calculation
  mutate(change = ...) |>        # compute the per-unit value
  left_join(other_df, by = "id") # bring in variables the first file never had

91.2 Demonstration

library(dplyr)
library(tidyr)

bp_wide <- read.csv("data/bp_visits_wide.csv")
baseline <- read.csv("data/baseline_enrollment.csv")

bp_change <- bp_wide |>
  pivot_longer(cols = starts_with("sbp_visit"), names_to = "visit", names_prefix = "sbp_", values_to = "sbp") |>
  filter(visit %in% c("visit1", "visit3")) |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = visit, values_from = sbp) |>
  filter(!is.na(visit3)) |>
  mutate(change = visit3 - visit1)

nrow(bp_change)
[1] 26
bp_change_with_age <- left_join(bp_change, baseline[, c("id", "age")], by = "id")

sum(is.na(bp_change_with_age$age))
[1] 10

10 of the 26 complete-case participants have no usable age — 9 simply aren’t in baseline_enrollment.csv at all (this visits study enrolled a broader group than the baseline file covers), and 1 (participant 13) is in baseline_enrollment.csv but has a missing age value there.

91.3 Explore

Try filter(bp_change_with_age, is.na(age)) and inspect which ids these are. Confirm 9 of them are ids outside baseline_enrollment.csv’s range entirely (using setdiff()), and 1 (id 13) is inside the range but has its own pre-existing missing age.

91.4 Solve the Problem

library(dplyr)
library(tidyr)

bp_wide <- read.csv("data/bp_visits_wide.csv")
baseline <- read.csv("data/baseline_enrollment.csv")

bp_change <- bp_wide |>
  pivot_longer(cols = starts_with("sbp_visit"), names_to = "visit", names_prefix = "sbp_", values_to = "sbp") |>
  filter(visit %in% c("visit1", "visit3")) |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = visit, values_from = sbp) |>
  filter(!is.na(visit3)) |>
  mutate(change = visit3 - visit1)

bp_change_with_age <- bp_change |>
  left_join(baseline[, c("id", "age")], by = "id") |>
  filter(!is.na(age)) |>
  mutate(age_group = ifelse(age >= 50, "50+", "Under 50"))

age_group_summary <- bp_change_with_age |>
  group_by(age_group) |>
  summarise(n = n(), mean_change = round(mean(change), 2)) |>
  arrange(age_group)

age_group_summary
# A tibble: 2 x 3
  age_group     n mean_change
  <chr>     <int>       <dbl>
1 50+           7       -1.86
2 Under 50      9       -8.56

Among the 16 participants with usable age and complete visit data, those under 50 show a much larger average blood pressure decrease (-8.56) than those 50 and older (-1.86) — the opposite of what you might have guessed, and a good reminder to report a surprising pattern rather than dismiss it, while still treating it cautiously (n=7 and n=9 are both small groups).

91.5 Interpret the Result

Answering your professor’s question required chaining nearly every tool from this level: pivot_longer() and pivot_wider() to align visit1 and visit3 for a per-participant change score, filter() to handle dropouts (Page 7), and finally left_join() to bring in age from a completely separate file — with 10 participants necessarily excluded from the final age-based comparison because that information simply doesn’t exist for them. The under-50 group’s larger average blood pressure drop is a genuine pattern in this small dataset, but with 7 and 9 participants per group, it’s a hypothesis to investigate further (Level 11), not a settled conclusion.

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting that joining across studies often means partial coverage, not full coverage. bp_visits_wide.csv and baseline_enrollment.csv weren’t guaranteed to describe the same participants — 9 of 26 simply had no baseline record at all, which is normal and expected, not a data error to “fix.”
  • Silently dropping unmatched rows without reporting how many. The move from 26 complete-case participants to 16 with usable age is a meaningful reduction — always state it explicitly rather than letting the final summary table’s row count speak for itself.
  • Treating a striking small-sample pattern as a confirmed finding. The under-50 group’s larger blood pressure drop, based on just 9 participants, is worth flagging and investigating further — not yet reported as if it were a validated result.

91.6 Practice Problem

Redo this pipeline joining in sex instead of age from baseline_enrollment.csv, and compare mean blood pressure change between male and female participants (among those with a usable sex value).

91.7 Challenge Problem

Redo the join using full_join() instead of left_join() between bp_change and baseline. Does nrow() of the result change? Explain what full_join() would add here that left_join() didn’t, given that baseline might contain enrolled participants who aren’t in bp_visits_wide.csv at all.

91.8 Key Takeaways

  • Real analyses often require reshaping and joining together — reshaping to compute a per-unit value, then joining to bring in variables that weren’t part of the original file.
  • Joining across two independently collected files frequently produces partial, not full, coverage — always report how many rows lack a match, and why that’s expected rather than an error.
  • A striking pattern found in a small, joined subset should be reported as a lead worth investigating, not treated as a final answer.

    4. There is no difference between the two here

  3. Predict the output. If 26 participants have complete visit data and 10 lack a usable age, how many remain in the final age-group summary?
