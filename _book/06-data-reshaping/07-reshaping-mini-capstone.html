85  Reshaping Mini-Capstone: Change Across Visits

The Problem

Your professor’s real question, at last stated plainly: “By county, how much did blood pressure change on average between the first and last visit?” Answering this needs visit 1 and visit 3 side by side in the same row so you can subtract them — but you also want the flexibility to pick “first and last visit” through filtering, rather than hardcoding column names like sbp_visit1 and sbp_visit3 directly.

Your Mission

Chain pivot_longer(), filter(), pivot_wider(), and mutate() together in one pipeline to compute a per-participant change score, then summarise it by county.

Think Before Coding

  • To compute visit3 - visit1 with mutate() (Level 5), do both values need to be in the same row, or can they be in different rows?
  • If you go to long format first, could you filter() down to just the two visits you care about, before pivoting back to wide to align them side by side for subtraction?

85.1 The R Concept

A reshaping pipeline often goes wide → long → (filter/analyze) → wide again: long format makes it easy to filter() down to exactly the visits you want, and pivoting back to wide afterward aligns the remaining visits side by side so you can compute a per-row difference with mutate():

df |>
  pivot_longer(cols = ..., names_to = "visit", values_to = "value") |>
  filter(visit %in% c("first_visit", "last_visit")) |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = visit, values_from = value) |>
  mutate(change = last_visit - first_visit)

85.2 Demonstration

library(dplyr)
library(tidyr)

bp_wide <- read.csv("data/bp_visits_wide.csv")

bp_change <- bp_wide |>
  pivot_longer(cols = starts_with("sbp_visit"), names_to = "visit", names_prefix = "sbp_", values_to = "sbp") |>
  filter(visit %in% c("visit1", "visit3")) |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = visit, values_from = sbp) |>
  filter(!is.na(visit3)) |>
  mutate(change = visit3 - visit1)

head(bp_change[, c("id", "county", "visit1", "visit3", "change")], 5)
  id      county visit1 visit3 change
1  1     Oakford    125    117     -8
2  2   Riverdale    123    129      6
3  3     Oakford    130    124     -6
4  4   Millbrook    117    116     -1
5  5   Millbrook    139    138     -1

Filtering out the 2 participants missing a visit3 reading before computing change (via filter(!is.na(visit3))) avoids NA change values contaminating the county averages next.

85.3 Explore

Try skipping the filter(visit %in% c("visit1", "visit3")) step and pivoting all three visits wide instead. Confirm you’d then need mutate(change = visit3 - visit1) regardless — visit2 would just sit unused in the result, since mutate() only references the columns you actually name.

85.4 Solve the Problem

library(dplyr)
library(tidyr)

bp_wide <- read.csv("data/bp_visits_wide.csv")

county_change_summary <- bp_wide |>
  pivot_longer(cols = starts_with("sbp_visit"), names_to = "visit", names_prefix = "sbp_", values_to = "sbp") |>
  filter(visit %in% c("visit1", "visit3")) |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = visit, values_from = sbp) |>
  filter(!is.na(visit3)) |>
  mutate(change = visit3 - visit1) |>
  group_by(county) |>
  summarise(n = n(), mean_change = round(mean(change), 2)) |>
  arrange(mean_change)

county_change_summary
# A tibble: 5 x 3
  county          n mean_change
  <chr>       <int>       <dbl>
1 Springvale      6      -10.17
2 Cedar Falls     4       -8.00
3 Millbrook       4       -3.50
4 Riverdale       6       -2.67
5 Oakford         6       -1.17

Springvale shows the largest average blood pressure decrease from visit 1 to visit 3 (-10.17), followed by Cedar Falls (-8.00) — though Cedar Falls’ figure rests on just 4 complete participants and Millbrook’s on only 4 as well (2 of Millbrook’s 6 participants dropped out before visit 3).

85.5 Interpret the Result

Every county in this subset shows a mean decrease in blood pressure from visit 1 to visit 3, consistent with the mild simulated treatment effect built into this dataset — but the size of that decrease varies meaningfully by county, from Oakford’s modest -1.17 to Springvale’s much larger -10.17. As always (Level 5), the smaller counties’ averages (Cedar Falls and Millbrook, both n=4 after dropout) deserve more caution than Oakford, Riverdale, or Springvale’s more stable n=6 estimates.

Common Mistakes

  • Computing change before removing missing visit3 values. mutate(change = visit3 - visit1) with a missing visit3 correctly produces NA for change — but if you forget to filter(!is.na(visit3)) (or handle it in summarise() with na.rm = TRUE), those NAs can silently propagate into a NA group mean.
  • Reshaping all three visits when only two are needed. Including visit2 in the pivot_wider() step doesn’t break anything, but it does create an unused column — filtering down to just the visits you need (as done here) keeps the pipeline focused and avoids confusion.
  • Interpreting a mean change without checking group size. A -8.00 mean drop in a county of 4 people carries much more uncertainty than a -1.17 mean drop in a county of 6 — reshaping and chaining code correctly is necessary, but not sufficient, for a trustworthy conclusion.

85.6 Practice Problem

Repeat this pipeline for weight instead of sbp (reshape the weight_visit1/weight_visit2/weight_visit3 columns, filter to visit1 and visit3, compute change, and summarise by county). Does weight show a consistent decrease across counties, similar to blood pressure?

85.7 Challenge Problem

Modify the pipeline to compute change between visit1 and visit2 instead of visit1 and visit3. Compare the resulting county rankings — does the county with the largest early change (visit1 to visit2) also have the largest overall change (visit1 to visit3)?

85.8 Key Takeaways

  • A common reshaping pattern is wide → long (to filter flexibly) → wide again (to align values side by side for a row-wise calculation).
  • Always resolve missing values from dropout or incomplete follow-up before they propagate into a downstream group summary.
  • A per-county (or per-group) summary is only as trustworthy as its underlying sample size — reshaping code correctly doesn’t guarantee the resulting numbers deserve equal confidence.

Knowledge Check

  1. Multiple choice. Why reshape to long format before filtering to specific visits, rather than just selecting wide columns directly?

    1. It’s required by R syntax
    2. It lets you filter by visit as an actual value, which generalizes better than hardcoding column names
    3. Long format uses less memory
    4. There’s no reason; both approaches are identical

  2. True or false. mutate(change = visit3 - visit1) will produce NA for any row where visit3 is missing.

  3. Predict the output. In county_change_summary, which county has the smallest-magnitude mean change (closest to zero)?

  4. Short answer. Why does Cedar Falls’ -8.00 mean change deserve more caution than Oakford’s or Springvale’s figures, even though all three are computed the same way?

Show answers
  1. b) It lets you filter by visit as an actual value (filter(visit %in% c(...))), which generalizes to any pair of visits rather than hardcoding specific column names.
  2. True. Arithmetic involving a missing value produces NA by default, matching the rule from every earlier level.
  3. Oakford, with a mean change of -1.17 (closest to zero).
  4. Cedar Falls’ mean rests on only 4 participants (this subset’s smallest county), making its average considerably less stable than Oakford’s or Springvale’s, both based on 6 participants.

## The R Concept

```r
df |>
  pivot_longer(cols = ..., names_to = "visit", values_to = "value") |>
  filter(visit %in% c("first_visit", "last_visit")) |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = visit, values_from = value) |>
  mutate(change = last_visit - first_visit)
```

- **Computing `change` before removing missing `visit3` values.** `mutate(change = visit3 - visit1)` with a missing `visit3` correctly produces `NA` for `change` — but if you forget to `filter(!is.na(visit3))` (or handle it in `summarise()` with `na.rm = TRUE`), those `NA`s can silently propagate into a `NA` group mean.
